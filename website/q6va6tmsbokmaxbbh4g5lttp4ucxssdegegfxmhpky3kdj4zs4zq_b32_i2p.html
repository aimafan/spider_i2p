War Against the Tech Machine

Are you viewing this website on a Microsoft or Apple Product (Windows, MacOS & iOS)? How about a Google Product (Android, Google Home)? Do you shop at Amazon or use Alexa Products like Echo? Or use any of Meta's Apps (Facebook, Instagram, Messenger)? I have linked each of their privacy policy to allow you to click on them. When I tell you what data they store on you, you can verify by using Ctrl+F (Or your keybind for your find on your browser). These companies have in common the following policies:

  • They keep data pertaining to your usage of their product. This can be benign, like crash information. however, it can be extremely intrusive, Like how Google collects your activity, like "People with whom you communicate or share content" and "Voice and Audio Information."
  • They keep metadata, data that is under the hood from the average user. This includes IP Addresses, Carrier information for mobile products, System activities, Date & Time, and in some fashion "Unique Identifiers" (From Google: IEMI-number of a mobile device).
  • They share data with your third parties, granted some are more restrictive than others.

I go much deeper in my Privacy Policy on the issues most of these major tech companies do with your data

What is "my" solution? To be honest, I understand the black pill that the only to fix this is to fight on the physical level. In all respect, I highly disagree on the conclusion, but I also have a separate perspective on this. If you're going to fight for your internet freedom, there will never be "peace". This means that the fight will continue on, between the surveillance groups and the (real) freedom fighters. This principle is an American principle: in order to maintain your freedom, you must fight for your freedom. the moment you become complacent, your freedoms will erode and be subverted. This is not to say, Go "Burn, Loot & Murder (BLM)", but to stamp out the bad actors by creating a massive cultural force that incentivises true privacy & anonymity technologies and disincentive bulk data collection & anti-privacy / anti-anonymity technologies. Yes, doing the physical part will be necesary to do (politically, culturally & philosophically). However the real fight is the technology space, from the root: the software dev culture. The Suckless Group is a great start, but I won't deny that their software is not user friendly. If we can bridge the gap in where we can provide both the technology & the education to the end users, thing will snowball into a direction that would be more desirable than the current direction we're heading towards.
